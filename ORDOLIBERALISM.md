# Ordoliberalism: The German School of Neoliberal Thought

Ordoliberalism, or "ordoliberalism," emerged in 1930s Germany as a distinct school of neoliberal economic thought. Unlike laissez-faire liberalism, ordoliberals advocated for a strong but limited state role in creating and maintaining market order. The movement was founded by economists like Walter Eucken, Franz Böhm, and Alexander Rüstow, who sought to reconcile economic freedom with social stability.

At its core, ordoliberalism emphasizes that markets require proper legal and institutional frameworks to function effectively. Proponents argued that competition should be seen as a dynamic "discovery process" rather than a static state. The state's primary role is to establish and enforce rules that prevent monopolies, ensure fair competition, and correct market failures.

This philosophy directly influenced Germany's post-war "social market economy" (Soziale Marktwirtschaft), which combined free enterprise elements with social welfare provisions. Unlike American neoliberalism that often favored deregulation, ordoliberalism maintained that markets need clear boundaries and robust antitrust enforcement to prevent concentration of economic power.

Ordoliberals were deeply skeptical of both unregulated capitalism and central planning. They believed that while markets were the most efficient allocators of resources, they required constant oversight to maintain their competitive character. This middle-ground approach aimed to preserve individual economic freedom while preventing the excesses that had contributed to the rise of totalitarianism in the 1930s.

Today, ordoliberalism remains relevant as it offers a framework for thinking about how to design market economies that are both efficient and socially sustainable, balancing freedom with responsibility in the pursuit of economic justice.
