Maxwell’s Equations¶

Eqn. (1.1) describes Gauss’ Law for electricity, which is basically a mathematical way of saying that the flux of E\bold{E}E fields across some surface ∂V\partial V∂V can be used to find the charge density inside the volume VVV. This is a fancy way of saying that static E\bold{E}E fields have no curl and are created entirely by static charges/charge densities ρ.

∇⋅E=ρϵ0\nabla \cdot \bold{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}∇⋅E=ϵ0​ρ​
(1.1)

Eqn. (1.2) is Gauss’ Law for magnetism, which says that magnetic fields B\bold{B}B are solenoidal/divergenceless and have no monpole sources (B\bold{B}B fields are created by current densities J\bold{J}J). Do not let the next equations fool you. The source of E\bold{E}E fields are static charges and the source of B\bold{B}B fields are current densities J\bold{J}J AKA moving charges.

∇⋅B=0\nabla \cdot \bold{B} = 0∇⋅B=0
(1.2)

Eqn. (1.3) is known as Ampere’s Law and relates changing magnetic fields with respect to time with the curl of the electric field E\bold{E}E, which is of course a differential relationship between the electric field and space. That describes the torsions the electrical field is capable of generating through accelerating electrical densities/potentials.

∇×E=−∂B∂t\nabla \times \bold{E} = - \frac{\partial \bold{B}}{\partial t}∇×E=−∂t∂B​
(1.3)

Eqn. (1.4) relates the torsions in the magnetic field (∇×B\nabla \times \bold{B}∇×B) due to changing electrical fields with respect to time (∂E∂t\frac{\partial \bold{E}}{\partial t}∂t∂E​) and current density J\bold{J}J. This equation allowed for the unification of electricity and magnetism into a single electromagnetic field and they both describe the forces and potentials generated by the motion of charged objects through space.

∇×B=μ0J+μ0ϵ0∂E∂t\nabla \times \bold{B} = \mu_0 \bold{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \bold{E}}{\partial t}∇×B=μ0​J+μ0​ϵ0​∂t∂E​
(1.4)

An equally important equation in our derivation of scalar light is Eqn. (1.5)

∇⋅J=−∂ρ∂t\nabla \cdot \bold{J} = -\frac{\partial \rho }{\partial t}∇⋅J=−∂t∂ρ​
(1.5)

And the mathematical identity for the curl of the curl, Eqn. (1.6)

∇×∇×A=∇(∇⋅A)−∇2A\nabla \times \nabla \times \bold{A} = \nabla (\nabla \cdot \bold{A}) - \nabla^2 \bold{A}∇×∇×A=∇(∇⋅A)−∇2A
